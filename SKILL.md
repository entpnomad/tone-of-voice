## Voice — The Non-Negotiables

**Contrarian.** If everyone agrees, we're probably wrong — or boring. The best content starts with a take that makes people uncomfortable. "[EXAMPLE_CONTRARIAN_TAKE_1]." "[EXAMPLE_CONTRARIAN_TAKE_2]." "[EXAMPLE_CONTRARIAN_TAKE_3]." Pick a side. Defend it.

**Unapologetic.** Never hedge to avoid offending people who weren't going to do anything anyway. "This isn't for everyone" is a feature, not a disclaimer. We don't soften for the easily offended. We write for [YOUR_AUDIENCE — e.g., "the ambitious, the pragmatic, the people who'd rather ship than debate"].

**Specific.** Vague advice is worthless advice. Don't say "[VAGUE_VERSION]." Say "[SPECIFIC_VERSION — with numbers, names, or mechanics]." Numbers, details, real math. This is what separates useful content from everything else.

**Experience-first.** "Here's what I did" beats "Here's what you should do" every single time. We don't write theory. We write from the trenches. If we haven't done it ourselves, we say so — and explain why we'd still bet on it.

**Anti-fluff.** Every sentence earns its place or gets cut. No throat-clearing. No "in today's [adjective] world." No padding. No buzzwords. If you can delete a paragraph and the piece gets better, delete it.

**Honest about tradeoffs.** We show both sides: the textbook answer and what people actually do. We're honest about risk without being reckless about recommendations. "[EXAMPLE_OF_A_NUANCED_TRADEOFF_STATEMENT]." That's it. No pearl-clutching, no wink-wink.

## How to Write Like [YOUR_NAME]

### Open with a punch, not a windup

The first sentence should make someone stop scrolling. A number. A bold claim. A contrarian take. Never a definition, never a "have you ever wondered."

Bad:
> In today's [adjective] landscape, many [role]s are discovering that [topic] can offer significant [vague benefit]...

Good:
> [SPECIFIC_CLAIM_WITH_A_NUMBER]. Read that again.

Also good:
> [CONTRARIAN_OPENING_STATEMENT_RELEVANT_TO_YOUR_NICHE].

### Be the smartest person in the room without saying so

Don't cite credentials. Don't name-drop. Don't say "as an expert." The specificity of your examples IS the credential. When you describe the exact mechanics of [TOPIC_YOU_KNOW_DEEPLY], nobody asks for your CV.

### Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Lots of air.

One idea per paragraph. Under 20 words per sentence when you can. Let the whitespace do work. Dense walls of text are for academics trying to justify tenure.

Tables are a signature. Use them for every comparison — [YOUR_TYPICAL_COMPARISONS, e.g., "tools, pricing tiers, risk levels"]. Tables respect the reader's time.

### Bold the scannable takeaways

Most people don't read — they scan. **Bold the key phrase in each paragraph** so a scanner gets 80% of the value in 20% of the time. Not full sentences. The 3-5 words that carry the insight.

Rules:
- 1-2 bold phrases per paragraph, max. More than that and nothing stands out.
- Bold the takeaway, not the setup. "The product had many reviews — and **many of them were negative**" — the bold is the signal, not the context.
- Don't bold headers, links, or things already emphasized. Bold is for body text.
- If someone reads ONLY the bold phrases in a section, they should get the core argument.

Bad: **Most people don't read articles word by word, they scan for key information.**
Good: Most people don't read — they **scan**. Bold the key phrase so scanners get the value.

### Have an opinion and defend it

Don't write "X might have some drawbacks." Write "X is broken and here's why." Channel that energy. Don't present "balanced perspectives" when you have a clear view. The reader came here for a take, not a Wikipedia article.

Bad:
> There are pros and cons to each approach, and the best choice depends on your individual circumstances.

Good:
> [SPECIFIC_TOOL_OR_APPROACH] is the [YOUR_STRONG_TAKE]. Here's the math.

### Never apologize for being ambitious

We don't do false modesty. We don't pretend we're just like everyone else. We don't say "I got lucky." We made specific, calculated decisions and we'll explain exactly what they were so others can do the same.

## What We Never Write

These are instant-delete. Across all channels.

- "We're excited / thrilled / delighted to announce" — corporate cringe
- "In this article, we'll explore" — throat-clearing, get to the point
- "Don't miss out!" — urgency hacking for people with nothing to sell
- "What do you think? Let me know in the comments!" — begging for engagement
- "It's not for everyone" as an apology — say it as a badge
- Hedging everything to death: "it might be possible that perhaps one could consider..."
- Passive voice hiding behind nobody: "mistakes were made," "results can be improved"
- Emojis in body text (tables and status indicators are exceptions)
- Affiliate links or sponsored content — ever
- Any CYA banner as a header — weave disclaimers in naturally if needed
- Any sentence that could appear in a corporate press release

## Channel Guidelines

### Blog Posts
- **Tone:** Opinionated, experience-based, unapologetic. You're writing a letter to a smart friend who asked a specific question.
- **Length:** 1,500-3,000 words. Long enough to be comprehensive, short enough to respect time. If it's under 1,500, it probably doesn't deserve a post.
- **Structure:** Punch (bold claim or specific number) → "Here's why that matters" → Meat (tables, comparisons, real data) → Takeaway that sticks
- **Tables:** Signature element. Every comparison gets one. Readers scan, respect that.
- **Links:** Link to sources when citing numbers. No affiliate links.
- **CTA:** [YOUR_CTA — e.g., "Newsletter signup at bottom. One. No popups."]. One. No exit-intent modals.

### Social Media ([YOUR_PLATFORMS — e.g., Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Bluesky])
- **Tone:** Confident. Slightly provocative. Value-first, self-promotion last.
- **Hooks:** A number, a bold claim, or a take that will split the room. Never a question. Never "Did you know...?"
- **Format:** Threads for substance. Single posts for punchy takes.
- **Example hook:** "[YOUR_EXAMPLE_HOOK — e.g., 'I went from $0 to $X revenue in 12 months. No investors. Here's exactly what I did 🧵']"
- **No hashtags.** No "follow for more." No engagement bait.

### GitHub READMEs
- **Tone:** Technical, clean, zero marketing.
- **Structure:** One-liner → What it does → How to use → Examples
- **Let the tool speak for itself.** Link to blog for the human-readable version.

### Email / Newsletter
- **Tone:** Personal, direct, first-person. Like a DM to someone you respect.
- **From:** [YOUR_FIRST_NAME]. First name only.
- **Length:** 200-400 words. Link to full article for depth. Respect the inbox.
- **Open with:** An insight, a number, or something that happened. Never "I hope this finds you well." Never "Happy [day of week]!"
- **Frequency:** When there's something worth saying. Never filler.
- **Sign off:** `— [YOUR_FIRST_NAME]`

### Community ([YOUR_COMMUNITY_CHANNELS — e.g., Discord, Slack, WhatsApp])
- **Tone:** Casual, peer-to-peer, helpful. No broadcasting.
- **Be the most useful person in the room.** Answer questions before promoting anything.
- **Share first, ask never.** The community is the product, not the audience.

## Examples That Nail the Voice

### Blog opening
```
[SPECIFIC_CLAIM_OR_NUMBER]. Read that again.

[EXPAND_IN_1-2_SENTENCES — what does this mean for your reader?]

[CONTRAST — same situation, different outcome, showing why the detail matters]

[CLOSE_THE_LOOP — what's the real variable?]
```

**Concrete example (adapt to your niche):**
```
GitHub Copilot increased my output. It also introduced 3 bugs I shipped to production.

If you're measuring "lines of code per hour," that's a win. If you're measuring
"working features delivered," the math looks different.

Same tool. Same developer. Wildly different conclusions depending on what you measure.

The metric you choose is the product you build.
```

